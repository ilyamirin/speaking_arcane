# Tarot Micro-Story Guidelines

This document defines how to write and rewrite spreads for `Speaking Arcane`.

The goal is not to list card meanings. The goal is to present each spread as a short dramatic scene:

- cards speak as characters inside one shared situation
- each card has a narrative function, not a generic interpretation
- the final male summary does not predict fate, it names the vector

## Core Format

Every spread should preserve the existing project structure:

- `introNote`: optional hook or framing line
- `dialogue`: short sequence of card lines
- `interpreterSummary`: the man's concluding read

The format remains:

1. setup or hook
2. tension or hidden pattern
3. turn or choice
4. exit, direction, or resource
5. male conclusion

## Non-Negotiable Rules

- Write a micro-story, not a glossary of symbols.
- Keep one clear arc per spread.
- Each line must move the scene forward.
- Avoid repetition between cards.
- Avoid fatalistic language.
- Avoid mystical filler and cosmic cliches.
- The conclusion must sound grounded, specific, and human.

## Target Rhythm

Use short, dense writing that can be consumed in about two minutes.

- `introNote`: 0-1 short line
- `dialogue`: ideally 4-5 lines, rarely 6
- `interpreterSummary`: 1 concise paragraph

Narrative balance:

- 30% setup
- 40% tension, conflict, or turning point
- 30% exit, direction, or conclusion

## Voice Model

Cards are not lecturers. They are characters observing the same emotional event from different angles.

Each card line should do one thing:

- name what has already begun
- expose the trap or tension
- push the scene toward a choice
- reveal the usable direction

The male conclusion should:

- compress the meaning
- speak plainly
- remove fog
- give orientation instead of prophecy

## Recommended Story Templates

Choose one template per spread. Do not mix several in one short reading unless there is a very strong reason.

### 1. Call -> Trial -> Gift

Use for movement, new feelings, threshold states.

- card 1: the impulse, invitation, or threshold
- card 2: the trap, fear, or test
- card 3: the hidden resource or next honest step

Best for:

- new romantic tension
- early-stage uncertainty
- situations where the answer is "yes, but not simply"

### 2. Tension -> Break -> Exit

Use for pauses, conflict, exes, emotional deadlocks.

- card 1: what is straining or no longer sustainable
- card 2: the breaking point or current fork
- card 3: where the decision leads

Best for:

- silence after intensity
- repeated patterns
- relationships at a turning point

### 3. Mystery -> Key -> Step

Use when the visible question hides a deeper one.

- card 1: the real issue behind the spoken question
- card 2: the clue or key
- card 3: the first concrete move

Best for:

- confusing mixed signals
- ambiguous intentions
- inner questions disguised as partner questions

### 4. Before -> Bridge -> After

Use for practical guidance and relational advice.

- card 1: current state
- card 2: bridge, shift, or behavioral hinge
- card 3: resulting state

Best for:

- "what now?"
- "how do I respond?"
- "what happens if I stop repeating myself?"

### 5. Archetype -> Lesson -> Direction

Use for introspection, intuition, and major arcana driven scenes.

- card 1: who the querent is being right now
- card 2: what that role is teaching
- card 3: where growth must move next

Best for:

- self-perception
- intuitive knowing
- spiritual or archetypal tension

## Spread Mapping For Current Content

Recommended default mapping for the current five spreads:

- `После свидания непонятно, захочет ли он увидеться ещё раз?` -> `Before -> Bridge -> After`
- `Почему он замолчал после тёплого начала?` -> `Tension -> Break -> Exit`
- `Это страх близости или ему просто не нужно продолжение?` -> `Mystery -> Key -> Step`
- `Стоит ли давать бывшему ещё один шанс?` -> `Tension -> Break -> Exit`
- `Что моя интуиция уже знает о нём, а я не решаюсь признать?` -> `Archetype -> Lesson -> Direction`

## Writing Constraints For `dialogue`

When writing `dialogue` lines:

- keep the total sequence tight
- avoid two cards saying the same thing with different adjectives
- let the cards disagree or correct each other
- make the third card change the energy of the scene

Good:

- card 1 opens the situation
- card 2 complicates it
- card 3 resolves, redirects, or sharpens it

Bad:

- card 1 explains
- card 2 explains again
- card 3 repeats the lesson in softer words

## Writing Constraints For `interpreterSummary`

The final male summary must:

- sound like a clean verdict, not a mystical chant
- not repeat every card in order
- not over-explain symbolism
- tell the listener what is actually happening underneath

Good ending shape:

- what this is really about
- what not to confuse it with
- what direction the querent should take

## Hooks For `introNote`

Use hooks sparingly. They should frame the scene fast.

Examples:

- `Этот расклад не о будущем. Он о выборе, который уже идёт внутри связи.`
- `Слушай не значения карт, а то, как они спорят о главном.`
- `Здесь важен не ответ мужчины, а момент, где всё упрётся в твою реакцию.`

## Language Rules

- Prefer verbs over abstractions.
- Prefer scenes over explanations.
- Prefer emotional precision over decorative spirituality.
- Say `он тянется, но держит себя`, not `здесь возможна амбивалентность`.
- Say `пора сделать шаг`, not `есть потенциал движения`.

## Avoid

- `Вселенная ведёт`
- `карты всё решили`
- `так должно было случиться`
- long suit-and-number explanations
- abstract card textbook language
- melodramatic doom
- fake certainty

## Editorial Check Before Publishing

Before accepting a spread rewrite, confirm:

- there is one recognizable narrative arc
- every card line has a distinct function
- the sequence can be understood in one pass
- the male conclusion adds clarity, not repetition
- the story feels spoken, not annotated

## Default Rewrite Formula

When rewriting an existing spread, use this fallback sequence:

1. Decide the best story template.
2. Assign a dramatic role to each card.
3. Reduce the dialogue to the minimum number of lines needed.
4. Make the third card create movement.
5. Rewrite the conclusion as a grounded male verdict.
6. Remove any line that only repeats a previous idea.
